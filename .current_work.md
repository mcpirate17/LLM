## Current Read

The real-word nano probe family is useful for diagnostics, but none of the
current probes should be wired as a leaderboard signal.

- `controlled_sentence_probe` is a report-only language-shape and step-curve
  diagnostic.
- `nano_blimp_v3` is a diagnostic for short-range real-word held-out behavior,
  but top-30 / 5-seed audit found structural 3-token template bias, especially
  against `token_merge_block`.
- `nano_controlled_holdout_v1` is the renamed Workstream C probe. Its
  failure-mode audit found near-zero top-50 correlation with PPL, false
  positives on weak screening-tier architectures, and saturation across the
  three calibration targets. Workstream C is done and withdrawn.
- The controlled-language probe family appears to measure speed of overfitting
  small curated corpora more than general language capability.
- Independent audit also flagged that the v3/cohort tuners train fresh-init
  architectures on random-token warmup rather than production weights, so these
  probes should be framed as architectural inductive-bias diagnostics, not
  trained-model language capability.
- Read-only real-metric audit over 7,813 leaderboard rows found token-merge is
  not penalized by existing real-text metrics; this confirms the short-sentence
  probes were structurally biased. Quick shipping path should use existing real
  metrics (`wikitext_score`, `tinystories_score`, HellaSwag/BLiMP diagnostics),
  not curated probe-time overfit.

**Why this test works where the previous controlled-language probe
family didn't:**

1. **Tim's design**: target frame + related-but-distinct sentences for

2. **Grade by slot-class + binding-subset membership**, not by exact

3. **Train fresh-init on the corpus directly** — no random-token

   budget on uniform random tokens.
4. **Multi-checkpoint eval** distinguishes "didn't converge" from
   "converged then mode-collapsed" — the latter is the dominant failure
   pattern for low-PPL archs.

### Top-5 reranking of full 30-arch cohort

Re-graded all 30 v4 runs by top-5 binding (more lenient than top-1):

- Ranks 1-19 (PPL 630-770): top-5 binding 1.00, top-5 adj count 3.0-5.0
- Rank 20: `13e8f410` topk_retrieval (PPL 1519): partial 0.80
- **Ranks 21-30 (PPL 25-106, the 10 "best PPL" archs): top-5 binding 0.00, top-5 adj count 0.0**

The 10 lowest-PPL architectures have ZERO adjectives in their top-5
predictions for any prompt. The mode-collapse to frequency tokens
(`was`, `the`) is total — not a top-1 ranking artifact.

### Token frequency check (v4 corpus)

```
the   515  (most common)
was   170  (failed archs predict this for everything)
sat   110
I     105
see   105
ran    92
```

Failing archs default to the 2nd-most-frequent token (`was`) for top-1
predictions, regardless of context. This is consistent with the
arch having learned a degenerate "predict the most frequent
context-conditional token" heuristic that fails when the prediction
position should produce an adjective.

### Final operating point (robust under tuning)

- Strict selection, n_adj_per_noun=3
- Corpus size 560 (v4 default), 80 A + 120 B + 80 C scaled to 160/240/160
- 1000 training steps, lr=1e-3, batch_size=32
- Top-1 grading with binding-subset check
- 5 held-out nouns / 15 total = 33% holdout

This config is robust under all tuning perturbations tested. The
discriminator (`top-1 binding ≥ 0.80`) gives 14/30 PASS, identifying
the same architectures regardless of test config tweaks.

### Conclusion of tuning study

Failing architectures are FUNDAMENTALLY UNABLE to do this binding test
at this scale. No hyperparameter combination tested rescues them. The
test reliably discriminates a clean architectural cap that's invariant
to test config — exactly what makes it a useful screening signal.

The `ebdd9a5c-8a3` partial unlock at lr=0.01 (top1 0.30 at 10K steps)
is the only borderline case; suggests `latent_attn_sparse_ffn` has the
capacity in principle but converges very slowly. Not worth special
handling.

### token_merge_block structural explanation (request from Tim)

The 4 token_merge_block entries that fail my binding test 0/144 across
the full matrix all have extremely thin motif lists:

| rid | ppl | op_count | motifs |
|-----|----:|---------:|--------|
| 574271ca-f37 | 37 | 8  | norm_layer, norm_rms, norm_rms, n_way_routing |
| 9fcd764b-08f | 29 | 12 | norm_layer, norm_rms, sparse_semi_structured |
| a3b532c7-4d8 | 63 | 5  | (zero non-norm motifs) |
| d49297a5-3bd | 66 | 10 | norm_layer, routed_ternary, norm_rms, norm_layer, norm_rms |

Pattern: low op count, dominated by normalization motifs, no attention,
no SSM, no positional mixing. The token_merge template merges tokens
along sequence dimension; synthesis-time slot filling produced these
specific instances with no real mixer in the merged-down slots.

**Why they get great PPL despite being thin**: token-frequency dominates
real-text loss when sequence-position mechanisms are absent. The model
learns to predict marginal `p(token)` excellently — that's enough for
PPL ~30 on WikiText. But it has no mechanism for positional binding,
so on the controlled `the {noun} was ___` task it predicts the marginal
mode (`was`, the 2nd-most-frequent training token) regardless of noun.

**690 total token_merge_block in the leaderboard**: 23 in PPL <50,
181 in PPL [50, 200). The "low PPL but no real learning" population
is substantial, and codex's quickcheck audit calling token_merge the
"best family by mean PPL" is consistent with this — it IS best at
real-text frequency prediction, but those are the architectures that
fail my binding test.
